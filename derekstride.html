The Sandbox Review: A In-depth Look into Blockchain’s New Community

 







Introduction

 

The World Wide Web has been a part of culture since its emergence in the late 1990s and has since overgone many changes, updates, and facelifts. As we loom upon Web3, so does the new dawn in the reaches that technology has and the opportunities inspired by this latest facelift. The Sandbox, a virtual Metaverse that lives within the new web 3.0, brings with it a heap of new opportunities we will explore in-depth in this Sandbox game review and guide.

The Sandbox is a wild-wild west kind of gameplay-focused frontier, with major partners ranging from Snoop Dogg, Atari & Adidas to Samsung and Coincheck, The Sandbox is a community-metaverse started on and powered by the blockchain. Here, the virtual LANDs, ASSETs, and businesses to NFT museums and 24/7 discos, the Sandbox game creates a rich opportunity cost to live out all the creative dreams you have ever had.

 

 

What is the Sandbox?

 

          The Sandbox is the best evolution of social media, moving from the adoration-driven models to one that people can truly create another life on in this gaming ecosystem. The Sandbox game is a community-driven, decentralized platform existing on the Ethereum blockchain and a space where users can own land, assets, or complete experiences as a character. One can make create and play games with features like Game Maker or get paid to create and share the creations you’ve made with the Game Maker Fund.

          The Sandbox also allows you to buy NFT land and monetize on it, whether it’s through hosting contests to voting in the Metaverse governance, or even staking SAND, the native coin to the Sandbox game. With only 166,464 available plots of land, the price is rising quickly as Sandbox takes on a life of its own, built for creators, by creators through 100% user-generated content.

 

 

How to Play the Sandbox

 

 

Sandbox is relatively simple to access; First, you’ll need to create a Sandbox account from sandbox.game on the blockchain. User profiles are created through your browser wallet, such as Metamask, then enter your email and create a password, before downloading the zip file, unpacking, and installing the game’s platform onto your computer, which should take up 300 MB. Within your profile on the site, you can customize your avatar, browse LAND available for sale and explore the many creator tools available.

How does the Sandbox work?

 

 

        Currently, the Sandbox beta has concluded, so it's unavailable to access but with Alpha season 2 making its way to market, let’s get you prepared. The idea of The Sandbox is a community-driven space to help fuel the creators while simultaneously rewarding the players and contributors, in fact over 5 million SAND was rewarded during season 1 alone! Exclusive NFT collections including Snoop Dogg, The Walking Dead, Smurfs, and Deadmau5 all call the Sandbox home, a legit list of both status and money.

          The Sandbox game is also a place for creators to thrive, allowing game makers to create free 3D games. The VoxEdit features also allow for a lot of control with the world, which can generate anything from people (NPCs) to animals, tools, and randomized objects. The Sandbox is essentially a place for everyone in the Metaverse to easily collaborate, sell, create and purchase NFTs, ASSETs, and LANDs.

 

 







How to make money on the Sandbox

 

 

          In the Sandbox ecosystem, creating income can be done in a variety of ways, paying the native token, SAND. You can design and profit from your own 3D games made possible with the Sandbox Game Maker or play premade ones ending with rewards. Any owned LAND can be rented to other players; use ASSETS to colonize it and increase its value, it may even make the Community news!

 

You can also create ASSETS with the VoxEdit feature and then upload them to the marketplace as NFTs. These can be many things from cars to animals to bikes and the income from them can range from a few SAND tokens to a few thousand SAND tokens. Ever forward-thinking, the ecosystem pays for all the Ethereum gas fees on behalf of the users, while 50% of the transaction volume is routed to The Sandbox Foundation, a promotor, and grantor for the metaverse.

You are primarily driven by recognizing and finding places and items throughout those experiences and quests. The more you complete, the more your level grows and the larger reward you get. Each experience hosts its own guru, objectives, and earning potential; and by the end of level 5, I earned 1000 SAND & 3 NFTs, worth maybe 0.025 ETH.

One day, we may have the Sandbox on our iOS iPhones, Android, and other mobile devices, enabling us to play and earn no matter where we are in the world, virtual Meta’s like The Sandbox can keep us forever connected. The idea is simple yet elegant, reward players to play, reward creators to create, and encourage LAND owners to make the most out of their investment; The Sandbox is a place where all, truly is one and where we all, are for each other.

  Poe was an author obsessed with discovering the causes for the downfall in the mental stability of man. He asserted not only can we not have perfect love without perfect loss, and vice versa; perfect love itself unattainable, an ideal common for the romantic writers. He exaggerates this ideal to be the perfect person you must have perfect love and loss both and also seemed to see familial structure of his day as well as society’s normality with religion as other vital catalysts for man’s mental instability. However, it seems that to Poe, full self-realization can never occur without madness and downfall. This seems to beg two questions: One, was this theme of downfall merely a technique utilized by Poe to liven up his work, in hopes of keeping his readers enticed by searching for the cause of this downfall with each new story? And two, was this theme of downfall a popular topic for writers of Poe’s day or was it a facet that made Poe the unique writer he is celebrated as today? Was it, in the end, this struggle for self-actualization that truly was the catalyst for the madness of man, according to Poe and his contemporaries?

  First, we must dictate what it is Poe considers perfect love, and that is namely, perfect loss. Man cannot understand what it is to fully love somebody until they lose that love they thought viewed as perfect. This is evidenced through many of Poe’s most famous works, a view of which will be broken down here.

  Ligeia is a story of a man driven into madness and melancholy following the loss of his childhood love,  he enters into a loveless marriage with a woman named Rowena. Eventually Rowena, like Ligeia, fall ill and eventually succumb to human mortality and perish. Many of Poe’s common themes are riddled throughout this work: the loss of a beautiful young woman, a man driven into mental instability, the dead coming back to life in the form of what the narrator wants most. These themes seem to be common in many of his most famous works and thus it begs the question, what is it in life that causes the downfall of men into mental frailty and eventually doom? The answer is the perfect woman that man can never have. In Ligeia, the description of the lady love herself is almost beyond human, she was the “beauty which passed into my spirit, there dwelling as in a shrine, I derived, from many existences in the material world, a sentiment such as I felt always aroused within me by her large and luminous orbs” (Poe 99). She is so perfectly beautiful he becomes entranced by her beauty, he is “possessed with a passion to discover” (Poe 99) the radiance she hides from the rest of the world. 

  She is so perfect for his wondrous nature he does not even ponder what the feeling of losing her would be until he actually does. This sends him into a deep plight of grief which is also a comment on how perfect Ligeia is, she is perfectly beautiful and causes the most perfect grief; “That she loved me I should not have doubted; and I might have been easily aware that, in a bosom such as hers, love would have reigned no ordinary passion but in death only, was I fully impressed with the strength of her affection” (Poe 101). The poem shifts from angelic descriptions of her enchanting beauty to ones of hopelessness following her death because his own internal self-worth only comes from Ligeia’s love. His marriage to Rowena is therefore lackluster because his happiness only rests in his memories. However, he would never be a fully realized adult in Poe’s vision because he needs to lose her to know the full weight of heartbreak.

  So, the loss of what the narrator of Ligeia has perceived as his true love drives him into madness, in fact, it decimates his chances of ever having a real relationship with another person. That being stated, his marriage to Rowena was doomed from the start because the loss of Ligeia has left him fully realized as to what love is and to him, that is Ligeia, not Rowena or anyone else in the world. His fixation over his love for his childhood love even seems to distract his full emotions when Rowena falls ill, which points even more to this ideal of one true love. In this work, Poe uses women for a catalyst to destroy men by being to perfect for them and only remaining with them forever if they forgo any chance at future happiness with a new suitor; that if you remain spiritually in debt to your ‘true love’ than maybe one day, you will get to be with them again. [Good idea about being spiritually in debt to the dead, perfect woman!]

  Annabel Lee, arguably one of Poe’s most famous works to date, is one of the best examples of this same perfect love is only perfect when coupled with perfect loss. It is love so perfect that it must lead to absolute mental destruction because love is meant to be perfect yet unattainable. According to The New World Encyclopedia, “Dark Romantics [Poe, Melville, Hawthorne, most notably] are much less confident about the notion perfection is an innate quality of mankind . . . individuals are prone to sin and self-destruction, not inherently possessing divinity and wisdom” (New World Encyclopedia Contributors). Annabel Lee was Poe’s most best depiction of man who loses his mental stability following the loss of true love, because unlike Ligeia where the love is resurrected, Annabel Lee’s narrator never gets his true, childhood love back but instead spends the rest of his life “bringing the dreams / of the beautiful Annabel Lee” (Poe 36-7). He will never be able to be as happy as he once was while he was in love, but is instead content sleeping next to her tomb for the rest of his days. This story is the ultimate dedication to perfect love and loss and in fact on of the only ones in which the narrator experiences both perfect loss and perfect love without ever getting his lost-love back. [Can he get Annabel Lee back from death? Does he really get Ligeia back? Or is it just some nightmarish version of her, or just his own opium-terror? I think it’s interesting to argue that Ligeia and narrator are reunited at end in some sort of celebration of their love, but you may need to make that case more clear in your analysis.]

  Berenice is a different taste of Poe, but one that is too riddled with all the fixings of Poe’s commentary on the causes of men to fall from stability. Instead of it being the loss of a woman, which directly causes Egaeus, in this case, to lose his mind we find a narrator whose mind already seems to be quite gone. He is obsessed with books and objects of imagination that seem to alleviate his mind from his apparent boredom of living in the “noon of manhood. . . still in the mansion of [his] father’s” (Poe 171). Egaeus is a gloomy man set to marry his cousin Berenice but unlike the narrator of Ligeia, Egaeus does not seek love he seeks escape and he does that through his imagination. He sees himself as inherently different from Berenice, saying “I, ill of health, and buried gloom – she agile, graceful, and overflowing with energy; hers the ramble on the hill side, mine the studies of the cloister, I living within my own heart and addicted body and soul to the most intense and painful meditation – she roaming carelessly through life with no thought of the shadows in her path” (Poe 172). Berenice is the narrators polar opposite; while Ligeia was to perfect for her narrator, Berenice is to free for hers. The women represent what the narrators long for but ultimately cannot have. Following Berenice’s death, the Egaeus loses this fixation of freedom which eventually leads him to mental downfall as he finds an object that both reminds him of her freedom but also alleviates his obsessive needs to have something that allows him to escape his life. Here, the woman also becomes the catalyst for man’s downfall, only because she represents an idea of self-freedom the narrator could never have himself instead of a romantic love; however, the idea that one needs someone else in order to achieve this idea of fully realized self-freedom also coincides with Poe’s theme of perfect loss and love in order to reach self-actualization.


  There is no doubt that Poe was one of the most celebrated writers of the Romantic period and of American literature in general. One of these things that makes him the ideal Romantic author was his ability to rebel against the popular literature he was raised in, namely the works and ideologies of the age of Enlightment. John Fawell discusses in his essay Romanticism, Spirituality, and the Contemporary University, “when I teach Romanticism I often turn to one passage from Edgar Allan Poe's short story ‘The Black Cat’ to get at what I think is a central idea of Romanticism, the passage in which Poe's narrator tries to understand, to explain to himself, why he has offered violence to his favorite pet cat, first gouging out its eye and finally hanging it [and] concludes that what lead him to hang the cat, what lead him to his ‘final and irrevocable overthrow,’ was the ‘spirit of perverseness” (Fawell 223). [quote too long, paraphrase idea] Poe describes this perverseness in The Black Cat as “the unfathomable longing of the soul to vex itself” (Poe 65) as an almost innate need that dwells within each of us. In other words, we are all built to eventually come to a downfall, so then if we accept the definition of perverseness in this instance to mean, “willfully determined or disposed to go counter of what is expected or desired” and we know Romantic’s were typically rebellious against the Enlightment, perverseness is a necessary element for Poe.

  Furthermore, if we accept this as a moral ground that Poe upheld, the notion that in order to be self-actualized we must know absolute acceptance and absolute rebellion, it would stand to reason why all of his work resulted in the downfall. One cannot possibly achieve perfect acceptance without rebellion against the established order and loss is common when one is attempting to create something new or at least contribute to the rising Romanticists, in his own case. This is poised to lead man into madness and Poe was no stranger to such, as evidenced by his long plethora of macabre works dedicated to the causes of the man’s madness. It was rebellious to write unhappy stories and this seemed to fuel Poe, someone who consistently felt dejected by society, as well as “greatly depressed by reversal of fortune, separation from loved ones, insecurity, and what to him was the frightful enigma of death” (Ostrom 44).

  Poe used downfall to keep readers interested, utilizing it as a hook device to keep readers coming back to his strange tales. In her essay The hidden god and the abjected woman in 'The Fall of the House of Usher,' Diane Long Hoeveler believes such a thing is just the case, that this keeps the reading searching for an overall meaning when the meaning of The Fall of the House of Usher is in fact abstract and up to interpretation. She asserts, “we follow any lead Poe drops in front of us, following it to blind passages and discover alas, only our mirror image staring back at us in mockery” (Hoeveler 385). The book that the narrator reads to Roderick before Madeline rises from the dead, Vigiliae Mortuorum, also raises this question of religion and its importance to Poe’s work, but specifically here to House of Usher. The rare Latin book highlights Roderick’s eccentric taste, but what this books presence in the story suggests according to Hoeveler is that the “Vigiliae Mortuorum tropes a religious history and ideology that have long been buried beneath the more extravagant gothic surface of both its own text and ‘The Fall of the House’, both which seemed to be written gothic and surely the reference to the book conveys specifically discursive nature of history in the text” (Hoeveler 386-7).  

  Essentially, society, which seems to rest upon religion in a major way, is by nature, discursive. Religion has always been surrounded by the mental downfall of not only individuals but also societies, yet religion presents itself as immortal. Poe seemed to recognize this, and this theme of religion as a catalyst for downfall seemed to even spill into other works (i.e. The Pit and the Pendulum). This is not to say he did not believe in God nor is that the assertion here, he just seemed to not see God as a pure entity free of responsibility for the pain of human life. His use of the twins of Roderick and Madeline in Fall of the House of Usher could even be alluding to Christ and the Virgin Mary, depicting the downfall of Christ as a result of religion’s failure to curb the sins of humanity thus religion itself is seen as a failure, if we fully recognize Hoeveler’s consensus as containing some sort of truth into Poe’s mind. If we do, then Roderick and Madeline are the over-exaggeration products of human ego, both wild hypochondriacs, doomed to downfall because of their family’s devotion to society, which is depicted by their wealthy status and thus social acceptance because of it, something Poe’s poverty never let him know personally. “Poe suggests that [like the human emotions of love and loss], history and religion can only be understood primarily as discourse systems, dialogical constructs that sacrifice male strength and creativity to the female- embodied powers of life and death, in other words, the cyclical nature of generation, that [Roderick and Madeline] no longer believe in themselves because they understand the fictional nature of both history and religion & consequently, like their creator, they only have the energy to self destruct” (Hoeveler 387-8). Downfall is the natural cycle of life people wants to ignore and Poe sought to naturalize within his texts. He truly makes the statement of the woman as the ultimate catalyst for downfall however, and Fall of the House does not escape the Poe technique because it is quite clear that “Roderick is the archetypal dispossessed male, the victim of his own narcissistic fantasies of the perfect feminine complement . . . Poe is the author of Roderick’s vision of cultural decay and historical meaninglessness” (Hoeveler 392-3).

  Loss in this century was a powerful commonality that could and often did guide, according to Poe, the course of human life; this mourning of lost-love then by large, becomes more dear than life itself. As Adam Bradford discusses in his essay Inspiring Death: Poe’s Poetic Aesthetics, “Annabel Lee,” and the Communities of Mourning in Nineteenth-Century America that the ritual of mourning in the American 19th century was very different than it is now, where as we do not commemorate death in the infinite manner that a writer in the Romantic period such as Poe would have; “In mourning portraiture and consolation verse, the deceased were frequently depicted enjoying a glorious afterlife which included reunion and continued association with previously departed family and loved ones. These affectively charged images, as I will show, also held out the possibility of a similar reunion for the deceased and the mourner in some future moment” (Bradford 76).  So, then Poe could not possibly have been the only Romantic writer that utilized this theme of downfall into his work because in fact themes of downfall into madness were prominent, although Poe was a leading figure in the macabre, downfall and perfect love/loss were most definitely not unique to Poe but greatly used amongst his contemporaries.
